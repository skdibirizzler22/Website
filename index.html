Evidence #2

🪶 The Lighthouse Keeper

Creative narrative text incorporating structural tension and tracking arcs.

Narrative Asset Grade: 83% (Level 4-)

Friday, August 13, 1999, a massive wind blasted across Stormy Bay, shaking houses as if they were made of paper. Huge waves crashed into rocks continuously, spraying water high into the air. Up on the mountain, the old lighthouse stood tall. It had been built in 1845, with old bricks and cracks running along the walls, and a lot of spiderwebs as if Spider-Man lived there. The wind howled around it, and the sea below roared like a dinosaur. Jonathan Reeves, its owner, had taken care of that lighthouse for many years, even though people said it was filthy and looked stupid, to him, it meant everything.

Jonathan was sitting in his cabin chilling, watching some Netflix when suddenly his phone buzzed loudly in his hand.

"SEVERE STORM INCOMING, HEAVY RAIN AND HUGE WAVES. STAY INDOORS."

He looked out the window right away. Outside, the sky had turned pitch black because of the dark clouds rolling in. The trees were screaming for dear life as massive wind came through them. Something felt off. Then his eyes moved to the lighthouse.

The light flickered.

It blinked once, then again, weaker each time. His stomach dropped. “No, no, not now!” his thoughts yelled at him.

He had to get straight to the plan quickly. He rushed outside, pulling his jacket tighter as the wind hit him. Rain started falling fast, soaking the ground and turning the dirt path into mud. He made his way to the shed, but as soon as he got there, he stopped dead.

A huge tree had fallen straight through it.

The wood was crushed, and pieces were scattered everywhere. The door hung loose, swinging slowly back and forth. For a moment, Jonathan just stood there, staring at the mess. Without fuel, the lighthouse would go dark. Without the light, ships would crash into the cliffs.

“THINK,” he said quietly.

Then he remembered the emergency gas can inside his cabin. He turned and ran back, nearly slipping as water spread across the floor. He searched quickly, moving things aside until he finally found it hidden in the back of his closet. He grabbed it and didn't waste another second. He ran back downstairs, putting on his waterproof boots and gloves. He tied a rope around his waist for safety because God knows what is going to happen next. The storm outside had gotten worse.

The second he stepped out, the wind pushed against him hard. Rain hit his face, cold and sharp. The path to the lighthouse was narrow, and now it was slippery too. Each step felt dangerous, but he kept moving forward because he wouldn’t give up.

“I will get these sailors safe in no time. I’m gonna make this right,” he said.

The lighthouse flickered again in the distance, its light dim but still there. It felt like it was holding on, waiting for him. Jonathan tightened his grip on the gas can and kept going.

When he reached the wooden bridge, a strong gust of wind punched him from the side. The gas can slipped from his hands and hit the ground, nearly falling at the edge. “NOOOOOOO!!!” he shouted.

It slid toward the edge. Jonathan dropped to his knees and reached out just in time to grab it before it fell. His heart was punching his chest like a boxer landing blows. “NOT TODAY,” he said, pulling it close.

He slowly stood back up and crossed the bridge, more careful this time. By the time he reached the lighthouse, he was soaked and freezing, but he didn’t stop.

He went inside, seeing the light was barely glowing and now he could hear the sailors' distant bells. Shadows moved across the walls as it flickered.

He rushed up the spiral stairs, holding onto the railing as his legs burned. At the top, he quickly poured the fuel into the tank. His hands were shaking from the cold and the pressure. “POUR FASTER! COME ON,” he muttered.

He lit it. For a moment, nothing happened. The storm outside continued to crash against the lighthouse. Then suddenly, the light came back.

A bright beam shot out into the darkness, slicing it like a sword and reaching far across the ocean.

Jonathan dropped to the floor, breathing heavily, letting the moment sink in. By morning, the storm had passed. The sky was clear, and the sunlight spread across the water. The town below was damaged, but the people were outside, safe.

Then they slowly made their way up to the lighthouse. “You saved us, Jonathan!!” one of them yelled.

Jonathan didn’t say much due to exhaustion. He just nodded and looked at the calm ocean. The lighthouse stood tall behind him, steady and strong. The townspeople offered to rebuild Jonathan’s shed as well. The lighthouse wasn’t just a building. It was hope. And somehow, so was he.

Analytical Essay Unit 4 Summative

Staying True To Yourself in The Hate U Give

By Rayan • May 29, 2026

A lot of people change the way they talk or act around different groups because they’re scared of being judged or left out. Blending in feels easier, but hiding who you really are builds stress and slowly tears down your confidence. In The Hate U Give, Angie Thomas shows this struggle through Starr Carter, a teenager who constantly switches up her personality depending on where she is and who she’s with. She lives in Garden Heights, a mostly Black neighborhood, but goes to Williamson Prep, a wealthy and mostly white school, so she feels like she has to split herself in two. After she witnesses a police officer kill her childhood friend Khalil, that double life becomes harder to maintain, and she starts to understand the cost of staying silent. The novel shows that staying true to yourself matters more than fitting in because pretending to be someone else wears you down, speaking the truth builds real courage, and accepting where you come from is the only way to gain confidence that lasts.

Trying too hard to fit in eventually forces you to act like two different people, and keeping that up is exhausting. Starr feels like she has to switch her entire personality depending on whether she’s in Garden Heights or at Williamson. At school, she avoids slang, watches her tone, and hides anything that might make her seem “too Black” to her classmates. As she explains, “I taught myself to speak with two different people and only say certain things around certain people” (Thomas 300). She isn’t just adjusting her behavior a little, she's cutting off real parts of herself just to feel safe. Instead of helping her fit in, this makes her feel more alone because she’s constantly monitoring everything she says and does. She ends up living two half-lives instead of one whole one. Thomas uses Starr’s struggle to show how draining it is to pretend to be someone you’re not. It doesn’t make life easier; it just makes you feel disconnected from yourself.

The pressure of switching between two identities also builds up over time. Starr wants to fit in at Williamson, but she also wants to stay true to her home and her community. The more she tries to keep those worlds separate, the more stressed she becomes. She’s always worried about slipping up—using the wrong word, reacting too strongly, or revealing something about her life that her classmates won’t understand. This constant self-editing shows how harmful it is when you feel like you can’t be your full self anywhere. Thomas makes it clear that pretending to be someone else might help you avoid judgment for a moment, but it eventually leaves you feeling lost.

Being honest about who you are is also what gives you real courage. After Starr watches Khalil get killed, she’s terrified to speak up. She’s scared of the media twisting her words, worried about gang retaliation, and nervous about how her school friends will react if they find out she was the witness. Staying quiet feels safer, so she hides behind her Williamson persona and tries to act like nothing happened. But eventually she realizes that silence isn’t helping anyone. She admits, “My silence isn’t helping Us” (Thomas 171). This is a turning point for her. She understands that by staying quiet, she’s letting other people control the story and letting Khalil be remembered as a stereotype instead of a real person. Once she decides to speak to the grand jury and the media, her fear starts to shift into purpose. Thomas shows that courage doesn’t appear out of nowhere—it grows when you stop hiding and choose to stand up for what’s right.

Starr’s bravery comes from honesty, not from trying to blend in. When she finally speaks out, she becomes stronger because she’s no longer carrying the weight of pretending. She realizes that her voice matters, and that telling the truth is more important than keeping other people comfortable. Thomas uses Starr’s growth to show that courage is built through action, especially when you decide to be yourself even when it’s risky.

Accepting where you come from is also the only real way to build confidence. At the beginning of the book, Starr treats her background like something she needs to hide, especially around her white friends like Hailey, who often makes racist comments without thinking. Starr worries that if people at Williamson know too much about Garden Heights, they’ll judge her. But as the story goes on, she starts to see her neighborhood differently. Garden Heights isn’t something to be embarrassed about—it’s a community full of love, strength, and history. It’s where she learned her values and where the people she loves most live.

By the end of the novel, Starr understands that her identity is something to claim, not hide. During the protests, she stands on top of a car and shouts, “Khalil, I’ll never forget, I’ll never give up, I’ll never be quiet. I promise” (Thomas 444). This moment shows how far she’s come. She’s no longer shrinking herself to fit in at school. She’s speaking loudly, proudly, and without apology. Thomas uses this scene to show that real confidence comes from accepting your roots and believing that who you are is enough.

Overall, The Hate U Give shows that trying to fit into a space that was never meant for you will only make you miserable. Starr’s journey proves that living a double life brings stress and confusion, while being honest with yourself is what actually makes you brave. By the end of the novel, Starr isn’t a completely different person—she’s just finally her whole self. She doesn’t feel forced to choose between Williamson Starr and Garden Heights Starr anymore because she realizes both sides are real and both belong to her. The biggest lesson Thomas gives is that you shouldn’t change who you are just to make other people comfortable. In a world that constantly tries to tell you who to be, staying true to yourself is one of the strongest choices you can make, because at the end of the day, the person you have to live with is you.

Works Cited

Thomas, Angie. The Hate U Give. Balzer + Bray, 2017.
